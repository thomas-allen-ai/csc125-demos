How to Play

Training data points appear one at a time. Your job is to draw a decision boundary that separates the two classes:

  • Red points = Class 0
  • Blue points = Class 1

Use the three sliders to position your boundary:

  • Angle — rotates the boundary (0° to 360°)
  • Sharpness — how confident the model is near the boundary (sharper = more confident)
  • Slide — moves the boundary left/right along the arrow direction

When training ends, your sliders lock and a test set appears (marked with yellow rings). Your score depends on how well your boundary generalizes to data you haven't seen.

Common mistake: Getting the classes reversed!
The blue arrow points toward where blue points should be. If you point the arrow toward red points, you'll classify everything backward and score near zero.

What the Colors Mean

The background gradient shows probability:

  • Deep red = high probability of Class 0 (red)
  • White = 50/50 (the decision boundary)
  • Deep blue = high probability of Class 1 (blue)

The boundary itself is where the model is least certain. As you increase Sharpness, the transition from red to blue gets faster — the model becomes more confident.

How It Connects to Logistic Regression

You're doing by hand what machine learning algorithms do automatically.

You are the optimizer.
In real machine learning, an algorithm called stochastic gradient descent (SGD) adjusts the parameters. Here, you're SGD. You see one point, adjust sliders, see another point, adjust again.

The formula at the bottom shows the actual logistic regression equation:

w₁x₁ + w₂x₂ + b → P(blue)

Your three sliders control these three parameters:

  • Angle → determines the direction of w₁ and w₂
  • Sharpness → determines the magnitude of w₁ and w₂
  • Slide → determines the bias term b

The model outputs a probability between 0 and 1. Points with P > 0.5 are classified as blue; points with P < 0.5 are classified as red.

Why Scoring Rewards Confidence

Your score is based on the probability assigned to the correct class, not just whether you got it right or wrong:

Score = 100 × average P(correct class)

If you correctly classify a point but only assign it 51% probability, you get 51 points. If you're 95% confident, you get 95 points. This is why Sharpness matters — being confidently correct beats being barely correct.

But beware: if you're confidently wrong, the loss goes through the roof. Real machine learning uses this same principle to train models.

The Key Insight

After a few rounds, you'll notice something: the boundary you fit to training data doesn't always work on test data. Sometimes a point lands right on the edge. Sometimes you got lucky; sometimes you didn't.

This is the essence of machine learning.
You're not memorizing the training data — you're trying to learn a pattern that generalizes. The gap between training performance and test performance is the central challenge of every ML system.

Real neural networks use the exact same loss function. A single-layer neural network with no hidden layer is logistic regression. What you're adjusting by hand is what backpropagation does automatically — millions of times per second.
